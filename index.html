About Our Research Group

We use geophysical approaches to help improve our understanding of the processes and features in the subsurface that impact groundwater movement, water resources, the safety of underground energy storage, and predictions of earthquake ground motions. Our aim is to: 1) determine the detailed structure of plate boundary regions and the interaction of growth faults and aquifer systems, 2) detect and locate microearthquakes near underground salt dome caverns, and 3) develop new methodologies for constraining the stress field that are important for capturing the physics of earthquake rupture.

We analyze and interpret geophysical datasets such as nodal and broadband seismic data, explosive source seismic data, airborne electromagnetic data, wellbore caliper and image logs, and laboratory measurements on cores.

Ongoing projects:

Indo-Burma Ranges and Bengal Basin
1) Bangladesh-India-Myanmar Array (BIMA), NSF-funded collaborative project to image the structure of the Indo-Burma subduction zone, where oblique convergence is attempting to subduct the massive Ganges-Brahmaputra delta. We installed 31 broadband seismic stations in Myanmar and 29 in Bangladesh. Recent results on the crustal structure of the Bengal Basin were presented at AGU 2023 and AGU 2024. Click here for our preprint.

2) We installed the Myanmar Universities Seismic Experiment (MUSE) nodal array in Yangon, the most populous city in Myanmar to study the variability of ground shaking from large earthquakes. Our site amplification study is published in Earthquake Spectra.

Southern California
3) Study of seismic attenuation beneath metropolitan Los Angeles using local earthquakes recorded by the dense BASIN nodal arrays aims to characterize the growth of earthquake sequences using fracture networks. Results were presented at AGU 2023. Click here for our Scientific Reports paper.

4) We investigate the accuracy of seismic velocity models used in ground motion prediction near the southern San Andreas fault by merging hi-res Earth models into regional models and simulating earthquakes using SPECFEM3D. Discrepancies between observed and synthetic seismograms provide useful information for improving future earthquake preparations in S. California. Plenary talk at the Statewide California Earthquake Center meeting 2023. Recent results published in The Seismic Record.

5) We carried out a volunteer-led seismic deployment (data available under network 8G) in October 2023 to image the structure of the San Fernando Valley in Southern California where the devastating 1994 magnitude 6.7 Northridge earthquake occurred. Results were presented at the 2024 Seismological Society of America Annual Meeting and 2025 AGU Fall Meeting. Two publications with a new 3D velocity model and earthquake detection using machine learning are available at Seismological Research Letters.

US Gulf Coast - Northern Gulf of Mexico
6) Our Southern Louisiana Micro-Seismicity (SOLAMS) project started in 2022 with the goal of understanding the influence of the major growth fault systems in southern Louisiana on subsidence and groundwater flow. Read more about this volunteer-led seismic experiment in our GSA Today article. Results were presented on the "Seismic Structure of the Tepetate-Baton Rouge Fault System and its Implications for Groundwater Flow in the US Gulf Coastal Plain" at AGU 2023.

7) We have developed new data collection strategies and machine learning algorithms to study microearthquakes and the processes affecting the mechanical integrity of underground storage caverns which can be detrimental for salt dome storage facilities and nearby communities. Our recent results were presented at the 2024 SAGEEP meeting. Click here for our G-cubed paper.

Southern Italy
8) This is part of the FURTHER project collaboration with Francesca Di Luccio and colleagues at the INGV. The overall goal of this project is to investigate the role of fluids in the preparatory phase of earthquakes in the Southern Apennines, Italy. We are contributing to the structural imaging by using receiver functions and autocorrelograms computed from recordings at broadband stations in the region. Results were presented at AGU 2024.


Other projects:

  • Our Investigating Seismicity in Louisiana (ISLA) project installed 10 broadband stations near Shreveport, Louisiana and aims to monitor seismicity and study the subsurface in NW Louisiana.

  • Direct measurements of in situ stress regimes in the Santa Barbara Channel, offshore Southern California using wellbore data from industry. Paper published in G-Cubed.

  • High-resolution 3-D P-wave velocity model for the Coachella Valley from explosive shots and local earthquakes to help improve ground shaking estimates.

  • Results from modeling of potential field data in the Gulf of California show serpentinized peridotite bodies such as found at magma-poor rifted margins are present beneath the rift.

  • BASIN project uses receiver functions from a dense nodal array to map the basement in the Los Angeles area.

  • Results from geodynamic models for the Gulf of California show bottom-driven shear and obliquity control the style of faulting and produces a wide plate boundary.
